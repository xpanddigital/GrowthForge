# Reviews as AI Trust Signals

When AI models decide whether to recommend a brand, they don't just look at what the brand says about itself. They look at what other people say. Reviews are one of the strongest third-party trust signals available — and most brands are dramatically under-invested in them.

## Why AI Models Weight Reviews

AI models are trained to value consensus. Reviews represent the purest form of consensus — hundreds or thousands of independent people sharing their experiences with a brand.

When ChatGPT is deciding between recommending Brand A (4.7 stars, 2,400 reviews across 5 platforms) and Brand B (3.8 stars, 12 reviews on one platform), the choice is clear. The AI model has far more evidence that Brand A delivers on its promises.

The signals AI models extract from reviews:

- **Volume** — More reviews = more data points = higher confidence
- **Rating** — Average rating across platforms indicates quality
- **Recency** — Recent reviews signal that the brand is actively delivering
- **Sentiment** — Beyond star ratings, the text content reveals specific strengths and weaknesses
- **Platform diversity** — Presence on multiple review platforms signals broader validation

## Which Platforms Matter Most

The review platforms AI models reference depend on the industry:

### Universal
- **Google Reviews** — Referenced by every model, especially Gemini and Google AI Overviews
- **Trustpilot** — Referenced frequently, especially for consumer brands

### B2B / SaaS
- **G2** — The dominant B2B software review platform. Heavily cited by AI models for software comparisons.
- **Capterra** — Similar to G2, strong in SMB software category
- **TrustRadius** — Referenced for enterprise software

### Local / Service Businesses
- **Yelp** — Still relevant for local service businesses
- **Google Reviews** — Dominant for local businesses
- **Industry-specific** — HomeAdvisor for home services, Avvo for lawyers, Healthgrades for doctors

<Callout type="info">
G2 reviews are disproportionately cited by AI models in B2B contexts. If you sell software or SaaS and you're not on G2 with a meaningful number of reviews, you're leaving AI visibility on the table.
</Callout>

## The Review Velocity Signal

Total review count matters, but review velocity — how many new reviews you're getting per month — might matter more. Here's why:

AI models weight recency. A brand with 500 reviews that are all from 2022 looks different from one with 200 reviews, 30 of which were posted in the last month. The second brand appears more actively used and currently relevant.

Healthy review velocity benchmarks:
- **Small business:** 2-5 reviews per month
- **Mid-market:** 5-15 reviews per month
- **Enterprise:** 15-50 reviews per month

## Sentiment Distribution

Star ratings are the headline number, but AI models also analyze review text for sentiment:

- **Positive reviews** that mention specific features or experiences are the strongest signal
- **Neutral reviews** (3 stars) are ambiguous and don't help or hurt much
- **Negative reviews** can actively harm AI recommendations — especially if they mention reliability issues, poor support, or unresolved problems

The ratio matters too. A brand with 90% positive and 10% negative looks trustworthy (nobody's perfect). A brand with 60% positive and 40% negative raises red flags.

## The Competitor Review Gap

This is often the most eye-opening metric in a review audit. If your top competitor has 2,000 reviews on G2 and you have 15, that gap is nearly impossible to overcome quickly — but you need to start closing it.

AI models don't need you to match your competitor's review count. They need you to cross a credibility threshold. For most categories:
- Under 10 reviews: Not credible enough to recommend
- 25-50 reviews: Minimum viable credibility
- 100+ reviews: Competitive
- 500+ reviews: Authoritative
