# Journalist Discovery

The best press release in the world doesn't matter if it lands in the wrong inbox. Finding the right journalists — people who actually cover your space and are likely to care about your story — is what separates campaigns that get coverage from ones that get deleted.

## How MentionLayer Finds Journalists

MentionLayer discovers relevant journalists through multiple signals:

### Publication Matching
Starting from your industry and keywords, MentionLayer identifies publications that cover your space. Then it finds the specific journalists at those publications who write about your topics.

### Recent Coverage Analysis
Rather than relying on static media databases, MentionLayer looks at who has recently published articles about topics related to your brand. A journalist who wrote about "CRM tools for agencies" last week is far more likely to cover your CRM story than one who covered it two years ago.

### Beat Identification
Journalists cover specific beats — technology, marketing, finance, etc. MentionLayer identifies journalists whose beats align with your brand's category, not just those who once mentioned a tangentially related topic.

## Relevance Scoring

Each discovered journalist gets a relevance score based on:

| Signal | Weight | What It Measures |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| Topic match | High | How closely their recent coverage matches your pitch |
| Publication authority | High | How much AI models weight the publication |
| Recent activity | Medium | How recently they published (active journalists > dormant ones) |
| Coverage frequency | Medium | How often they write about your topic area |

A journalist at a major publication who wrote about your exact topic last month scores highest. One at a minor blog who covered a tangentially related topic a year ago scores lowest.

<Callout type="tip">
Prioritize journalists with recent, topically relevant coverage over those at bigger publications. A niche industry journalist who covers your exact space will almost always deliver higher-value coverage than a generalist at a major outlet who might not respond.
</Callout>

## Building Your Media List

For each campaign, build a targeted list of 20-40 journalists:

### Tier 1 (5-10 journalists)
Highly relevant beat, authoritative publication, recent coverage of your topic. These get your most personalized pitches.

### Tier 2 (10-15 journalists)
Good topic match, solid publications, but less recent or less specific coverage. These get slightly more templated pitches with personal touches.

### Tier 3 (10-15 journalists)
Broader industry coverage, secondary publications. These get efficient pitches that still reference their work but with less deep personalization.

## The Importance of Personalization

"Spray and pray" — blasting the same pitch to hundreds of journalists — doesn't work. Journalists receive dozens of pitches daily and can spot a mass email instantly.

Effective pitches reference:
- A specific article the journalist recently wrote
- Why your story matters to their specific audience
- A unique angle they haven't seen from other pitches

MentionLayer generates these personalized elements automatically by analyzing each journalist's recent articles and identifying connection points to your pitch.

## Managing Your Journalist Database

Over time, you build relationships with journalists who cover your space. MentionLayer tracks:

- **Response history** — Who opened, replied, published
- **Coverage quality** — Did they write a feature, a mention, or ignore you?
- **Beat changes** — Journalists change publications and beats. Track where they are now.
- **Preferred contact method** — Some prefer email, some Twitter DMs, some LinkedIn

This institutional knowledge compounds over time. After 3-4 campaigns, you'll have a core list of 10-15 journalists who reliably cover your stories.

## Ethical Outreach

Good journalist relations follow these rules:

- **Never lie.** Don't claim exclusivity unless you're offering it. Don't inflate numbers.
- **Respect "no."** If a journalist declines, don't send the same pitch again.
- **Provide value.** Offer exclusive data, early access, or expert sources — not just self-promotion.
- **Follow up once.** A single follow-up after 48-72 hours is fine. More than that is pushy.
- **Be available.** If a journalist responds, reply quickly with whatever they need.
